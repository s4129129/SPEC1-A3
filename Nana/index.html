OVERCONSUMPTION /
PURCHASING HABIT

Overconsumption has shifted clothing from a necessity into an engine of climate change.

Research confirms that tonnes of annual textile waste makes the fashion industry responsible for roughly 8–10% of global carbon emissions (Niinimaki et al. 2020).

In America specifically, textiles represent approximately 5.8% of all municipal solid waste in the United States. In 1960, this figure was significantly lower; the volume has grown from 1.7 million tons to over 17 million tons annually as "trend chasing" became a consumer habit (Environmental Protection Agency 2023).

By prioritizing trends and cost over sustainability, modern consumer culture has become a deadly agent of climate change.

PRODUCTION

The Invisible Threat: Microplastics

The Global Infiltration: A World Pre-Polluted
Microplastics are light enough to be carried by wind, meaning we inhale synthetic fibers from our clothes every day, even in remote environments (Kalogerakis 2025). Once shed from polyester or nylon garments during washing, these fibers enter the water cycle, where they are ingested by plankton and can bioaccumulate through the seafood and crops we consume. Current estimates suggest adults may ingest or inhale the equivalent of one credit card's worth of plastic every single week (Savchuk 2025).

The Climate Feedback Loop: Disruption of the Carbon Pump
Microplastics can impair the ability of phytoplankton to photosynthesize, reducing the amount of CO₂ the ocean can absorb from the atmosphere (Moon 2025). When zooplankton ingest plastic fibers, their waste becomes more buoyant. Instead of sinking and trapping carbon on the seafloor, this waste remains near the surface, releasing carbon back into the atmosphere (Estrela 2025).

Breaking the Cycle: The Power of Secondhand
Synthetic fiber production is fossil-fuel intensive; buying secondhand avoids the greenhouse gas emissions associated with creating new polyester (Dennings 2025). New synthetic clothes shed the highest concentration of microplastics during their first few washes, while secondhand items have often already undergone those initial high-shed cycles.

WASTE
MANAGEMENT

A Broken System

  • Only 1% of textiles are recycled back into clothing (Rawshot 2026).
  • Synthetic blends like cotton-polyester can't be efficiently separated or recycled (Arisa 2025).
  • Up to 40% of clothing "donated" to the Global South is trash that lands in foreign landfills (Schirrer 2026).

By the Numbers

  • One cotton t-shirt = 2,700 litres of water (Earth.Org 2026).
  • Textile dyeing causes 20% of global industrial water pollution (Mizrachi & Sharon 2025).
  • 85% of textiles are thrown out yearly; polyester takes 200+ years to decompose (Roadrunner 2021).
  • 500,000 tons of microplastics shed into oceans from synthetic clothes each year (Earth.Org 2026).

Why We Keep Buying

  • Clothing consumption is up 400% in two decades (Earth.Org 2026).
  • Items are worn under 7 times before being tossed (Earth.Org 2026).
  • 55% of Gen Z uses Buy Now Pay Later for ultra-fast fashion (Akepa 2026).

Why Secondhand Works

  • Wearing a garment 9 extra months cuts its footprint by 20–30% (WRAP 2023).
  • One used item saves 25 lbs of carbon on average (Reinhart 2026).
  • A secondhand cotton shirt saves 2,700 litres of water (Earth.Org 2026).
  • Thrifting builds a circular mindset, breaking the disposable habit (MacArthur n.d.).

TRANSPORTATION

Fast fashion depends on constant, high-speed global shipments of goods to maintain rapid turnover, which places significant strain on the environment.

From raw material sourcing to manufacturing to final delivery, the constant movement of fast fashion garments majorly increases emissions, especially if companies favour air transport for speed. Global fashion is responsible for around 10% of total carbon emissions, with transportation playing a major role in that footprint (United Nations Environment Programme 2023).

Some studies show that production and transport can account for up to 91% of a garment's total emissions, depending on the product lifecycle (MDPI 2024).

Transportation is often invisible to consumers—yet it remains one of the largest hidden contributors to fast fashion's environmental impact. The faster clothes move, the higher the environmental cost.

THRIFTING /
SECONDHAND

Consumerism

Over 100 billion garments are made every year, and over 92 million tons of clothing become waste (Sommke5 2024). Fast fashion takes part in excessive production, but consumers are also responsible for purchasing with awareness. People possess 60% more clothing than 15 years ago, while those clothes are only used at about 50% of their potential (Sommke5 2024).

Benefit of Thrifting

Thrifting gives people access to more unique items that are still in good condition at an affordable price (Shaw 2024). It helps the environment by reducing demand, while also reducing financial burden because people can access items that would otherwise be more expensive (Shaw 2024).

The lower the demand, the lesser it damages the climate

Mass consumerism leads to massive production of products requiring non-renewable materials, and those materials are being used at a very fast rate (Goodwill Industries International 2024). Toxic chemical emissions from fashion factories lead to environmental deterioration and affect nearby habitats and communities (Goodwill Industries International 2024).

It starts with your lifestyle

Many young people prefer thrifting over buying new outfits or products from official stores (University of Colorado Boulder 2026). Thrifting can save money, develop personal taste without relying heavily on trends, and build a stronger sense of responsibility to consume with intention rather than impulse (University of Colorado Boulder 2026).

SDG 13 — Climate Action: Take urgent action to combat climate change and its impacts.
“The greatest threat to our planet is the belief that someone else will save it.” (Sustainability and Acciona n.d.)
Robert Swan's 1989 statement perfectly captures the dangerous mindset fueling modern "throwaway" culture. When the environmental cost of production is deferred to corporations or future waste-management technologies, individuals disengage from the impact of their own consumption, allowing the cycle of fast fashion and overproduction to persist.

This passive attitude contradicts the core of sustainable living, which requires an urgent shift toward a circular economy and conscious consumerism. The environmental crisis isn't just sustained by industrial inaction; it is fed by a global pattern of convenience where each person assumes their single "new" purchase is insignificant.

Thrift culture emerges as a powerful act of resistance, encouraging people to shift from mindless buying to personal accountability. It serves as a direct reminder that the most sustainable product is the one that already exists.

Join us in the journey of thrifting culture and buying secondhand: the most powerful way to protect our planet is to realize that our own small choices at the checkout are what will save it.
